Performance Guides

Data-driven articles for runners and athletes who train with pace, heart rate, and wearable data — not generic fitness advice.

How to Use These Guides

PaceWOD is not a generic fitness blog. Every article here is written for athletes who train with data — pace splits, heart rate zones, watch metrics, race results — and want to know when to trust the number and when to question it. We built the site around a simple problem: your Garmin, COROS, and Apple Watch give you more data than most training plans know how to interpret. Our guides close that gap.

What Makes PaceWOD Different

Most fitness content stops at "run easy" or "do intervals." We start where those instructions break down. Why does your easy run drift into zone 3 on a hot day? Why does Garmin predict a 3:45 marathon from a hilly 10K? Why does VO2 max on your watch drop after your best race week? Each guide names the formula, device behavior, or physiological mechanism behind the confusion — then gives you a decision rule.

Content falls into three layers that work together:

  • Guides explain concepts — zone 2 physiology, VO2 max pacing, EMOM structure, race-day fueling
  • Training plans apply those concepts across 8–16 weeks with explicit paces, HR ranges, and adjustment rules
  • Tools produce the numbers the guides reference — zone boundaries, race predictions, calorie estimates, WOD templates

A guide without a tool gives theory without inputs. A tool without a guide gives numbers without context. Use both.

Recommended Workflow

Step 1 — Establish baselines. Before reading training plans, calculate your zones with the Zone 2 Calculator and HR Zones Calculator. Enter a recent race into the Race Predictor. These outputs become the paces and HR ceilings referenced throughout the plans.

Step 2 — Match guide to goal. Training for a half marathon? Read the Easy Run Pace Guide first — most plans fail because easy days are too hard, not because quality sessions are too easy. Chasing VO2 max? Read the wearable accuracy article before obsessing over weekly Garmin updates.

Step 3 — Validate with real sessions. Run one week as written. Compare watch HR against perceived effort. If tempo HR exceeds 88% max before the prescribed duration ends, the plan's pace targets need adjustment — not your commitment. Our guides explicitly say when to scale back.

Step 4 — Re-calibrate monthly. Race or time trial every 4–6 weeks. Update the calculators. Trends matter more than snapshots — a zone 2 pace that drops 8 sec/km over six weeks at the same HR is real progress, even if the watch VO2 max graph looks flat.

Expert Note

No PaceWOD article assumes lab-grade accuracy from consumer wearables. We treat wrist HR, estimated VO2 max, and race predictors as useful inputs with known error bars — not ground truth. When a guide cites a number, it includes the typical deviation (±3–8 bpm HR, ±3–5 ml/kg/min VO2, ±2–4 min race prediction over half marathon distance). Train with the range, not the point estimate.

Who This Content Is For

Recreational to competitive runners who own a GPS watch and want structured training without hiring a coach. CrossFit athletes who also run and need conditioning work that does not destroy leg freshness before long runs. Data-curious athletes who ask "why does this number disagree with that number?" instead of averaging them and hoping for the best.

If you prefer motivational posts about "listening to your body" without defining what that means in bpm or sec/km, this library will feel overly technical. If you have ever argued with your watch about whether you were really in zone 2, you are in the right place.

Honest Limits

We cite established methods — Daniels, Friel, Riegel, MET tables, Firstbeat whitepapers — and show our math. We also state what those methods cannot do. A race predictor does not know your fueling strategy. A zone calculator does not know your cardiac drift on hills. An EMOM template does not know your shoulder mobility. Guides tell you what to do when the formula and reality diverge.

How we write

Every PaceWOD article explains why a number differs between your watch, a calculator, and lab testing — then tells you which one to train with. We cite methods (Riegel, Daniels, Friel, MET), show real scenarios, and link to the tool that produced the estimate.
