Tempo Runs: Purpose, Pace, and Workouts

Updated July 2026 · 12 min read

Tempo running is controlled discomfort. It's the pace you could hold for roughly 60 minutes in a race — hard enough to raise lactate, sustainable enough to run for 20–40 minutes continuously. Tempo work raises your lactate threshold, the ceiling that determines how fast you can race from 10K to marathon.

What Tempo Training Does

At tempo intensity, your body produces lactate at the same rate it clears it. Train here regularly and that threshold shifts upward — paces that once felt hard become manageable. Tempo runs bridge the gap between easy aerobic work and VO2 max intervals.

  • Raises lactate threshold — Delays the pace at which legs burn and breathing spikes
  • Builds race-specific fitness — 10K and half marathon pace live near tempo effort
  • Teaches pacing discipline — Holding steady effort for 20+ minutes is a skill

Finding Your Tempo Pace

Tempo pace typically falls between 10K race pace and 15–20 seconds per mile slower. Heart rate lands in zone 4 — roughly 80–90% of max. Use the Race Predictor or Pace Calculator to set targets from a recent race.

Quick Reference by Distance

  • 5K focus: Tempo at current 10K race pace
  • 10K focus: Tempo at goal 10K pace or slightly slower
  • Half marathon focus: Tempo at goal HM pace
  • Marathon focus: Tempo at marathon pace to 15 sec/mile faster

Check zones with the HR Zones Calculator if pace feels ambiguous.

How Hard Should It Feel?

Tempo is "comfortably hard." You can speak in short phrases but not full sentences. Breathing is rhythmic and deep. By the final minutes, you should want the workout to end — but you shouldn't be sprinting. If you're gasping by minute 10, you started too fast.

Common Mistakes

Starting too fast. The first 5 minutes should feel almost too easy. Settle in before pushing.

Running tempo every week without easy days. One tempo session per week is enough for most runners. Surround it with easy running — see our easy run pace guide.

Confusing tempo with intervals. Tempo is continuous sub-maximal effort. Intervals are shorter reps with recovery — different stimulus, different purpose.

Building Tempo Into Your Plan

Start with 15–20 minutes of tempo once per week. Add 5 minutes every 2–3 weeks until you reach 30–40 minutes. Advanced runners can run two tempo sessions in peak weeks, but never back-to-back. Pair tempo days with easy running before and after for best results.

Worked Example: Setting Tempo Pace From a 10K Result

Profile: Male, age 42, recent 10K race: 44:10 (4:25/km). Goal race: half marathon in 12 weeks. Max HR 182, lactate threshold HR estimated at 168 bpm (from 30-min time trial). Zone 4 via HR Zones Calculator: 163–173 bpm.

Tempo pace calculation: Race Predictor projects half marathon at ~1:34:00 (4:27/km). Tempo target: 10K pace + 5 sec/km to 10K pace − 5 sec/km → 4:20–4:30/km. First session: 20 min continuous at 4:27/km after 15-min warm-up.

Session data: Lap splits 4:29, 4:26, 4:27, 4:25 km — even enough. HR: 162 → 166 → 169 → 170 bpm (normal cardiac drift). RPE 7/10, speaking in 4–5 word chunks. COROS wrist HR lagged ~8 sec; chest strap confirmed zone 4 entry at minute 6, not minute 3.

Progression week 8: Same 4:27/km tempo for 30 min, HR peak 172 bpm (was 170 at 20 min). Week 12 dress rehearsal: 3 × 10 min at 4:22/km with 2 min jog — half marathon goal pace embedded in cruise intervals. Use the Pace Calculator to convert min/km targets if your watch displays min/mile.

Garmin, COROS, and Tempo Intensity: Avoiding Zone Confusion

Tempo sits in the narrow band between "too easy to adapt" and "too hard to sustain." Wrist HR makes this band narrower still. Garmin and COROS both smooth HR over 3–7 second windows; at tempo pace on rolling roads, reported HR can sit 5 bpm below true physiological load on uphills and 5 bpm above on recoveries — making it look like you nailed zone 4 when effort was actually bipolar.

Lactate threshold auto-detect: Garmin updates LTHR from hard runs; a misclassified long run at moderate effort can drag threshold down and shrink zone 4. COROS EvoLab threshold pace updates similarly. After setting tempo pace from a race, manually lock threshold in watch settings for 4–6 weeks so auto-updates don't rewrite your targets mid-block.

Pace vs HR during tempo: In cool conditions with fresh legs, tempo pace may land at 78% max HR — low zone 4. In heat, the same pace hits 88%. Pace is the workout prescription; HR is the governor. If HR exceeds zone 4 ceiling by 5+ bpm in warm weather, slow 5–8 sec/km rather than aborting — the stimulus is effort duration, not a pace tattoo.

Structured workout sync: Uploading tempo workouts to Garmin/COROS helps lap pacing, but default alerts on "pace too slow" encourage early surges. Set wide pace bands (±10 sec/km) for the first 5 minutes. Check post-run training load (Garmin aerobic TE ~4.0, COROS training load 80–120 for 30-min tempo) only for week-to-week comparison, not session-to-session perfectionism.

Expert Note

The most common tempo failure is starting at interval pace because the first 5 minutes "felt easy." Tempo should feel almost conservative at minute 3 and honestly hard at minute 18. If you're gasping at minute 8, you ran 10K race pace, not threshold pace — reset using race predictor minus 15–20 sec/mile, not minus zero.

FAQ

How is tempo different from threshold intervals?

Tempo is continuous — 20–40 min unbroken at steady effort. Threshold intervals (e.g., 3 × 10 min) include short recovery jogs that let lactate clear partially, allowing slightly faster reps or more total time at intensity. Both raise LT; tempo teaches pacing patience, intervals teach handling repeated discomfort.

Can I do tempo runs by heart rate alone?

Yes, if you know threshold HR. Cap the session at LTHR to LTHR + 3 bpm. Without a measured threshold, HR-only tempo is guesswork — derive pace from a recent race via the Race Predictor, then note the HR you actually hit for future HR-only days.

One tempo per week or two for half marathon training?

Most recreational half marathoners thrive on one weekly tempo (building 20 → 35 min) plus one interval or hill session. Two tempos suit advanced runners with 50+ mpw and proven recovery metrics (stable resting HR, good sleep). Never schedule tempo back-to-back with long runs or VO2 max work.

Tempo vs Threshold vs Cruise Intervals

Coaches and watch apps use these terms interchangeably — they are not the same workout. All three target lactate threshold adaptation, but structure, recovery, and sustainable pace differ. Picking the wrong format is how runners end up running 10K race pace and calling it tempo.

Numeric HR example (same athlete): Max HR 190, lactate threshold HR 168 bpm (from 30-min time trial). Zone 4 via the HR Zones Calculator: 163–173 bpm. Goal half marathon pace: 4:30/km.

  • Tempo run20–40 min continuous at ~4:27–4:32/km · HR 165–172 bpm · no recovery breaks · RPE 7/10 · teaches pacing patience and sustained lactate steady-state
  • Threshold intervals3 × 10 min at ~4:22–4:27/km · HR 168–174 bpm on reps · 2–3 min easy jog between · RPE 8/10 on final rep · slightly faster per rep because partial lactate clearance during rest
  • Cruise intervals4 × 8 min at ~4:25/km · HR 166–171 bpm · 90 sec jog recovery · total time at intensity often exceeds a single 25-min tempo · lower per-rep speed than threshold intervals, higher density than classic tempo

Decision framework: Use continuous tempo when you need to learn race rhythm — half marathon and marathon prep, flat courses, steady pacing discipline. Use threshold intervals when continuous tempo feels mentally brittle or you want 30+ total minutes at intensity without holding one pace block. Use cruise intervals as a bridge: more threshold time than a 20-min tempo, less neuromuscular cost than VO2 max reps.

Common misclassification: A runner holds 175 bpm for 15 minutes, rests 2 minutes, repeats twice, and logs "tempo run." That is threshold intervals — the HR exceeded LTHR + 5 bpm on reps, and recovery jogs dropped lactate between efforts. Garmin may label the whole session "tempo" based on average HR; your training log should reflect structure, not watch taxonomy.

Weekly placement: One of these formats per week for most runners. Do not stack cruise intervals on Tuesday and continuous tempo on Thursday — both stress the same system. Sandwich any threshold work with easy zone 2 days per our easy run pace guide. Verify pace targets with the Pace Calculator if converting between min/km and min/mile.

Expert Note

Threshold intervals at 4:22/km with 2-min jogs often produce lower session average HR than a 30-min tempo at 4:30/km — yet the interval session can be harder to recover from. Compare next-morning resting HR and easy-day compliance, not just average workout HR, when choosing between formats.
