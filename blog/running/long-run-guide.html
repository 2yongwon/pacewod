The Long Run: Benefits, Pacing, and Fueling

Updated July 2026 · 8 min read

The long run is the anchor of distance training. One weekly session where time on feet matters more than pace — building the aerobic capacity, muscular endurance, and mental resilience that shorter workouts can't replicate. Whether you're training for a 10K or a marathon, the long run earns its place on your calendar.

But "go long and go easy" is incomplete advice. The athletes who progress fastest treat the long run as a monitored session: they track cardiac drift, compare watch pace to perceived effort, and know exactly when 90 minutes requires mid-run fuel. This guide covers all three — not just how far to run.

Why the Long Run Works

  • Glycogen adaptation — Teaches muscles to store and use fuel more efficiently
  • Capillary and mitochondrial density — More oxygen delivery at every pace
  • Tendon and bone resilience — Repeated impact builds structural durability
  • Mental rehearsal — Comfort with discomfort transfers directly to race day

How Long Is Long Enough?

Duration matters more than distance. General targets by race goal:

  • 10K: 60–75 minutes
  • Half marathon: 75–105 minutes (peak)
  • Marathon: 90–150 minutes (peak), time-based not mile-based

Increase long run duration by 10–15 minutes every 1–2 weeks, with a cutback week every 3–4 weeks. Follow a structured plan like our 12-week half marathon plan for safe progression.

Pacing the Long Run

Most of the long run should be easy — zone 2 heart rate, conversational effort. The classic mistake is running long runs at moderate pace, which adds fatigue without extra benefit. A second mistake: trusting average pace on the watch while HR drifts upward across the session.

Cardiac Drift: The Metric Most Runners Ignore

Cardiac drift (HR decoupling) measures how much heart rate rises at the same pace as glycogen depletes and heat accumulates. On a well-executed easy long run, HR from minute 10 to minute 60 should stay within ~5% at constant pace on flat terrain.

Example: A runner holds 5:45/km for 90 minutes. HR starts at 138 bpm (true zone 2) and finishes at 152 bpm at the same pace — that's ~10% drift. Either the opening pace was too fast, conditions heated up, or hydration was insufficient. Compare with our Zone 2 Running Guide for decoupling benchmarks.

Garmin and COROS show pace and HR on the same screen, but they don't flag drift automatically. Export the activity and compare average HR by 20-minute segments — or use a chest strap for cleaner data on runs over 75 minutes. Wrist optical sensors lag and smooth spikes, which hides early drift signals.

Expert Note

On humid days, easy pace may drop 30–45 sec/mile while HR stays in zone 2. That is correct pacing — not fitness loss. Chasing your dry-weather pace on an 85°F morning is how long runs become tempo runs by accident.

Easy Long Run

Entire run at easy pace. Use the Zone 2 Calculator to stay honest. This is the default for base-building phases.

Progression Long Run

Final 15–30 minutes at marathon or half marathon pace. Teaches running fast on tired legs — critical for late-race performance.

  • Warm up50 min easy (zone 2)
  • Finish20 min at goal half marathon pace
  • Cool down5 min easy jog or walk

Fueling Basics

Runs under 75 minutes rarely need mid-run nutrition if you've eaten beforehand. Beyond that, plan to refuel with math, not guesswork:

Example fueling plan (90-minute long run): A 70 kg runner burns roughly 700–900 kcal depending on pace (estimate with our Calories Calculator). Glycogen stores support ~60–90 minutes of moderate effort. Starting the run fed (40–60g carbs 2 hours prior), then taking 30g carbs at minute 45 and minute 70 (gel or sports drink) prevents the pace-HR disconnect that looks like fitness loss but is actually fuel depletion.

  1. Before: Light meal 2–3 hours prior — carbs, moderate protein, low fiber and fat
  2. During: 30–60g carbs per hour (gel every 30–45 min, or sports drink)
  3. After: Carbs plus protein within 60 minutes to kickstart recovery

Long Run Logistics

Route planning. Out-and-back or loop courses with water access. Don't commit to 90 minutes without a bathroom option.

Time of day. Run when race conditions match — if your half is at 8 AM, practice long runs at 8 AM.

Recovery after. The day after a long run should be rest or very easy movement. The adaptation happens during recovery, not during the run itself.

When to Cut a Long Run Short

Sharp pain (not general soreness), chest tightness, dizziness, or extreme heat warrant stopping. A missed long run is recoverable; pushing through injury is not. Repeat the previous week's duration rather than jumping ahead after a missed session.

Long Run vs Race-Day Fitness

A strong long run does not guarantee race performance. It confirms aerobic durability — the ability to hold effort for 90–150 minutes. Race performance also depends on threshold development (tempo work), pacing discipline, and course-specific factors. Use the Race Predictor only with honest recent inputs, and read why race predictors are often wrong before setting marathon goals from a single long-run result.

FAQ

Should I use pace or heart rate on long runs?

Heart rate on easy long runs, pace on progression finishes. HR accounts for heat, hills, and fatigue; pace is better when you're deliberately running the last 20 minutes at marathon effort.

Is walking during a long run a failure?

No. Walk breaks on hot days or hilly routes often keep HR in zone 2 better than forcing run pace. The adaptation comes from time on feet at appropriate intensity, not continuous running at moderate effort.

How do I know if my long run was too fast?

Check next-day resting HR (elevated 5+ bpm), leg heaviness on a planned easy day, and cardiac drift percentage. Two of three signals mean back off duration or pace next week.

Weekly Integration: Where the Long Run Fits

The long run is not an isolated Sunday effort — it sits at the end of a load sequence. Tempo and interval sessions raise your lactate threshold and VO2 max ceiling; easy runs between them absorb that stress. The long run tests whether that combination actually holds up under duration. Run it too close to hard days, or stack moderate effort all week, and the long run becomes a fatigue dump instead of an aerobic builder.

Spacing rules that work for most half-marathon and marathon blocks: Place the long run 24–48 hours after your last quality session (tempo or intervals), never the day after. Keep at least two true easy days in the week — zone 2 HR per the Zone 2 Calculator, not "easy-ish" moderate pace. One tempo per week is enough when long-run duration is climbing; two quality sessions plus a 90+ minute long run is an advanced load, not a default.

How tempo and easy runs feed the long run: Tempo teaches sustained sub-maximal effort — useful if your long run includes a progression finish. Easy runs keep weekly volume high without adding glycolytic debt. If Tuesday tempo leaves Thursday legs heavy, Saturday's long run will show it as HR drift above 8% or pace collapse in the final third. That is data, not failure — it means the week was sequenced wrong or recovery was insufficient.

Worked Week Example (Half Marathon Prep, 45 mpw)

  • MondayRest or 30 min walk — post-weekend reset
  • TuesdayEasy 8 km, zone 2 (HR 138–148 bpm)
  • WednesdayTempo: 15 min warm-up + 25 min at threshold pace + 10 min cool-down
  • ThursdayEasy 6 km recovery jog — must stay zone 2 despite post-tempo freshness temptation
  • FridayRest or 40 min easy zone 2 + 4 × 20 sec strides
  • SaturdayLong run 90 min easy; final 15 min at goal half marathon pace (progression)
  • SundayRest — non-negotiable after progression long run

What success looks like: Wednesday tempo peaks at 168–172 bpm (zone 4). Saturday opens at 140 bpm, finishes progression at 158 bpm — drift under 8% on the easy portion. Sunday resting HR within 2 bpm of baseline. If Saturday HR hits 165 bpm in the easy section alone, Wednesday tempo was too close or Thursday was not easy enough.

Expert Note

CrossFit athletes stacking MetCons on Friday evening before a Saturday long run often see zone 3 drift by km 8 — not because the long run pace is wrong, but because glycogen and neuromuscular recovery were spent elsewhere. Treat the long run as the weekly audit of your entire load, not just running mileage.
