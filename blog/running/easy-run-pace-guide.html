How to Find and Run Your Easy Pace

Updated July 2026 · 6 min read

Easy running is the most important session most runners get wrong. Run too fast and you never recover from hard workouts; run too slow and you still get most of the benefits. The goal is a pace you can sustain while holding a full conversation — usually 60–90 seconds per mile slower than 5K race pace.

The hard part is not finding a formula. It is executing easy pace when your watch, training partners, and ego all push faster. This guide gives three validation methods plus a worked example showing when pace and heart rate disagree — the exact scenario that breaks most recreational training weeks.

Why Easy Pace Matters

Easy runs build capillary networks, strengthen tendons, and accumulate weekly volume without crushing your nervous system. Elite marathoners spend 80% of training at easy effort. Recreational runners often invert that ratio — and wonder why they're always tired.

  • Recovery — Flushes metabolic waste between quality sessions
  • Volume — Low stress allows more total miles
  • Adaptation — Aerobic enzymes and mitochondria develop at easy intensity

Three Ways to Find Easy Pace

1. Heart Rate (Most Reliable)

Easy running falls in zone 2 — roughly 60–70% of max heart rate. Calculate your range with the Zone 2 Calculator or the full HR Zones Calculator. On hilly or hot days, slow down to stay in zone even if pace drops dramatically.

2. The Talk Test

You should speak in complete sentences without pausing to breathe. If you can only manage a few words, you're going too fast. This method requires no equipment and works surprisingly well once you're honest with yourself.

3. Pace Relative to Race Fitness

As a starting point, easy pace is typically:

  • 1–1.5 min/mile slower than 5K race pace
  • 45–75 sec/mile slower than 10K race pace
  • 30–60 sec/mile slower than half marathon pace

Plug a recent race time into the Pace Calculator to get reference paces, then add the buffer above.

Worked Example: When Pace and HR Disagree

Profile: 5K PR 22:30 (4:30/km race pace). Calculator suggests easy pace around 5:20–5:40/km. Zone 2 HR range (Karvonen, max 192, resting 52): 138–150 bpm.

Tuesday (12°C, flat): 5:35/km feels conversational, HR 142 bpm. Easy day executed correctly.

Thursday (28°C, humid): Same 5:35/km feels harder, HR 156 bpm — now zone 3. Correct action: slow to 5:55/km or insert 30-second walk breaks to return HR to zone 2. Wrong action: hold pace because "easy pace is 5:35" — this turns the recovery run into a moderate session and compromises Saturday's long run.

This is why heart rate should override pace on easy days. Pace is an output; HR reflects physiological cost under current conditions. Read Zone 2 vs Zone 3 for what happens when easy days drift gray-zone week after week.

Expert Note

Garmin "easy run" suggested pace uses recent training history — if you've been running too hard, the watch normalizes a inflated "easy" pace. Reset by two weeks of HR-capped easy running, then recheck whether suggested pace drops.

Common Easy-Run Mistakes

Ego pacing. Running with faster friends on recovery days turns easy runs into moderate efforts. Run alone or tell your group you're keeping it easy.

Chasing a number. Easy pace varies daily based on sleep, stress, heat, and hydration. A pace that felt easy Tuesday may require walking breaks Thursday — that's normal.

Skipping easy runs. They're not filler. They're where fitness compounds. Protect them like tempo days.

Sample Easy-Run Week

  • MondayEasy 40 min (zone 2 HR)
  • WednesdayTempo or intervals (hard day)
  • ThursdayEasy 35 min (zone 2 HR)
  • SaturdayLong run — first 60 min easy, optional last 15 min moderate
  • SundayOptional recovery jog 20–30 min or rest

When Easy Feels Hard

If staying in zone 2 requires a shuffle or walk breaks, your aerobic base needs work — not more intensity. Commit to 4–6 weeks of honest easy running. Your easy pace will naturally quicken as fitness improves. Read our Zone 2 Running Guide for a deeper dive into aerobic training.

Easy Pace by Training Phase

Base phase (8+ weeks from race): 100% of easy runs in zone 2. Pace is irrelevant.

Build phase: Easy runs still zone 2, but long run may include last 15–20 min at marathon pace — not the same as making Tuesday's recovery run "moderately easy."

Taper: Easy pace often feels unusually fast at lower HR — neuromuscular freshness, not proof you should race tomorrow.

FAQ

My easy pace is barely slower than marathon pace. Is that normal?

For experienced marathoners with high aerobic economy, yes — the gap narrows. For 5K–10K specialists, a 60–90 sec/mile buffer is typical. Compare HR, not just pace spread.

Should I use the talk test or HR if they conflict?

Trust HR when heat, hills, or fatigue elevate cost. Trust talk test on flat cool days when wrist HR lags. Best practice: both must pass on easy days.

Can I run easy pace on a treadmill?

Yes, but treadmill pace often feels easier (no wind, stable surface). Set 1–2% incline and still cap HR — don't chase outdoor pace numbers indoors.

Decision Framework: When Pace Overrides HR (and Vice Versa)

Easy pace is not one number — it is a range bounded by physiology on a given day. Use this priority order:

  1. Heat index above 26°C or humidity above 75%: HR cap wins. Slow until Zone 2 is restored, even if pace drops 30–45 sec/km below your calendar average.
  2. Elevated resting HR (>8 bpm above 14-day average): HR cap wins. Indicates incomplete recovery, illness, or stress. Shorten the run or walk if needed.
  3. Flat, cool conditions with normal resting HR: Both talk test and HR should pass. Pace is a useful secondary check — if 5:30/km feels conversational at 141 bpm, no adjustment needed.
  4. Downhill or tailwind segments: Pace will look artificially fast. Ignore pace spikes; maintain HR ceiling.
  5. Post-hard-day recovery runs: HR cap wins for the first 20 minutes. If HR settles, pace may naturally rise in the second half — that is fine.

Numeric check: A runner whose 14-day easy average is 5:32/km at 143 bpm should not panic when a 5:48/km day at 140 bpm appears after poor sleep. The slower pace with lower HR is the correct easy run — not evidence of lost fitness.

Garmin and COROS Easy-Pace Limitations

Both platforms offer "easy run" guidance, but the underlying models create common misinterpretations.

  • Garmin suggested recovery pace: Derived from recent training load and performance estimates. If you have been running too hard for weeks, the algorithm normalizes an inflated "easy" pace. Two weeks of strict HR-capped running usually resets the suggestion downward by 10–20 sec/km.
  • COROS pace zones vs HR zones: COROS can display pace-based zones from race predictions independently of HR zones. Pace zone 2 and HR zone 2 may not align on hot or hilly days — always defer to HR when they conflict.
  • Optical HR delay on easy starts: Wrist HR reads low for the first 5–8 minutes of jogging. Starting too fast to "get HR up" pushes the first kilometer into Zone 3 before the sensor catches up. Walk 2–3 minutes before jogging to avoid this.
  • Auto-pause and lap averaging: Stoplights and crossings create pace gaps that make post-run average pace look slower than continuous effort felt. Judge easy execution by HR time-in-zone, not average pace alone.
  • Running power (Garmin/COROS): Power targets on easy days can help on hills where pace is meaningless, but consumer running power estimates vary ±5–8% between devices. Use power as a secondary check, not a replacement for HR on flat easy runs.

Configure HR zone alerts at your Zone 2 ceiling in both apps. When the alert fires more than twice in 10 minutes, slow down immediately — regardless of what suggested easy pace displays on screen.

Additional FAQ

How much slower should easy pace be than marathon pace?

For most recreational runners, easy pace sits 45–90 sec/km slower than marathon pace. Highly aerobically trained marathoners may show only 30–45 sec/km gap. Compare HR, not just the pace spread — a narrow gap at low HR indicates good economy, not a reason to speed up.

Does cadence matter on easy runs?

Not as much as intensity. Easy-day cadence naturally runs 5–10 spm lower than tempo work. Forcing higher cadence on recovery days increases metabolic cost and can push HR into Zone 3. Let cadence settle organically unless a coach has prescribed drills.

Should I add strides to easy runs?

4–6 × 15–20 second relaxed accelerations after an easy run are fine and do not negate the aerobic benefit. Full interval sessions belong on separate hard days — not appended to every recovery run.
