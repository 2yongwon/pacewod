12-Week Half Marathon Training Plan

Updated July 2026 · 14 min read

The half marathon rewards patience and volume. Twelve weeks gives you time to build a long run to 90 minutes, develop lactate threshold with tempo work, and arrive at the start line fresh. This plan targets runners who can already run 60–75 minutes continuously.

Starting Point

Ideal candidates have finished a 10K and run 20–30 miles per week. Your long run should be at least 60 minutes before week 1. Estimate goal pace with the Race Predictor using a recent 10K or 5K result.

  • Weekly mileage starting point: 20–30 miles
  • Long run baseline: 60–75 minutes
  • At least one tempo or interval session in recent training

Setting Goal Pace From Data

Half marathon goal pace should come from recent race evidence, not aspiration alone. Enter a 10K from the last 8 weeks into the Race Predictor — Riegel formula with exponent 1.06 typically maps a 48:00 10K to roughly 1:42–1:44 half marathon if your aerobic base supports the distance.

Worked example: 10K in 47:30 → predicted half ~1:41:30 (4:48/km). Tempo runs target that pace. Long-run race-pace segments start 10–15 sec/km slower (4:58–5:03/km) in weeks 5–6, then progress to full goal pace by week 8. If tempo at 4:48/km pushes HR above 88% of max for the full 35 minutes, the goal is aggressive — extend the timeline or adjust to 4:52/km.

Expert Note

Garmin race predictions often assume perfect conditions and full marathon-specific endurance. A 10K-based half prediction may be 3–5 minutes optimistic for runners who have never completed a 90-minute long run. Trust the predictor for pace direction, then validate with tempo HR before committing to a start-line goal.

Plan Overview

Five runs per week: three easy, one quality (tempo or intervals), one long run. Easy mileage fills the gaps — keep it honest with heart rate or the talk test. See our easy run pace guide if easy days feel too hard.

Weekly Mileage Progression

Total volume climbs gradually — never more than 10% week over week except during planned recovery weeks (week 4 drops ~15%). A runner starting at 25 miles/week might progress like this:

  • Weeks 1–325 → 27 → 28 miles — foundation
  • Week 424 miles — recovery cutback
  • Weeks 5–828 → 30 → 32 → 33 miles — build to peak
  • Weeks 9–1230 → 26 → 22 → race week ~12 miles

Easy runs should sit in zone 2 (roughly 65–75% max HR or conversational effort). If Tuesday tempo leaves Wednesday easy run at 80%+ max HR, Tuesday was too hard or Monday was not easy enough. Use the Zone 2 Calculator to set honest easy-day ceilings.

Weeks 1–4: Aerobic Foundation

Gradually extend the long run and introduce weekly tempo segments. No single week should increase total mileage by more than 10%.

Typical week (Weeks 1–4)

  • MonEasy 40 min
  • TueTempo 20–25 min at HM pace
  • WedEasy 35 min
  • ThuEasy 40 min
  • SatLong run — 70 min (W1), 75 min (W2), 80 min (W3), 65 min easy (W4 recovery)

Weeks 5–8: Build Phase

Long runs reach 85–95 minutes with the final 20–30 minutes at half marathon effort. Tempo runs extend to 30–40 minutes. Add one interval session every other week.

What "Half Marathon Effort" Means on the Watch

HM effort is not a feeling — it is a narrow physiological band. For most trained runners, half marathon pace corresponds to 82–88% of max HR, or roughly 95–105% of lactate threshold heart rate (LTHR). A runner with max HR 182 and LTHR 168 might tempo at 165–170 bpm and hold 158–162 bpm during long-run race-pace segments before cardiac drift adds 3–5 bpm in the final 15 minutes.

Heat, hills, and glycogen depletion shift pace at fixed HR. A 90-minute long run finishing at goal pace might require 4:50/km in March and 5:05/km in August at the same heart rate. Pace the effort by HR on hot days; pace by splits on cool, flat race-rehearsal runs.

Expert Note

The last 25 minutes at HM pace during a 90-minute long run is the highest-value session in this plan. It teaches fueling under fatigue, pacing discipline, and mental tolerance for discomfort at race intensity. Do not skip it because the first 65 minutes felt slow — that fatigue is the point.

Week 6 example

  • MonEasy 45 min
  • TueTempo 35 min at HM pace
  • WedEasy 40 min
  • Thu4 × 1 mile at 10K pace, 2 min jog
  • SatLong 90 min (last 25 min at HM pace)

Week 8 example (peak week)

  • MonEasy 45 min
  • TueTempo 40 min at HM pace
  • WedEasy 40 min
  • ThuEasy 45 min
  • SatLong 95 min

Weeks 9–12: Sharpen and Taper

Volume drops while intensity stays touchable. Long runs shorten; race-pace segments stay short and sharp.

Week 10

  • MonEasy 40 min
  • Tue3 × 1 mile at HM pace
  • WedEasy 35 min
  • SatLong 75 min

Week 11

  • MonEasy 35 min
  • TueTempo 20 min at HM pace
  • WedEasy 30 min
  • SatLong 60 min

Week 12 (race week)

  • MonEasy 25 min
  • Wed3 × 1K at HM pace
  • FriRest
  • SunHalf marathon

Fueling for Long Runs

Practice race nutrition on every long run over 75 minutes. Aim for 30–60g of carbs per hour — gels, chews, or sports drink. Your gut is a trainable system; race day is not the time to experiment.

Fueling Math for Race Day

A 70 kg runner at 4:50/km half marathon pace finishes in ~1:42 and burns roughly 1,100–1,300 kcal (estimate with our Calories Calculator). Glycogen stores alone cover ~90 minutes at moderate effort — a half marathon exceeds that for most athletes.

Practical protocol: Pre-race meal 2–3 hours out (60–80g carbs, low fiber). During the race: gel at km 8 and km 14 (30g each), or 150 ml sports drink every 20 minutes at aid stations. Total mid-race intake: 60–90g carbs. Practice this exact timing on the week 8 long run — note GI response, not just leg feel.

Race-Day Execution

Start conservatively. Miles 1–3 should feel almost too easy. Settle into goal pace through the middle miles, then run the second half equal to or faster than the first (negative split). Use the Pace Chart and Calories Calculator to plan pacing and mid-race fueling.

Split Strategy With Numbers

For a 1:45:00 goal (5:00/km), the first 5K at 5:05–5:08/km costs only 15–25 seconds but preserves glycogen for km 15–21. Checkpoints:

  • 5K25:30–25:45 — should feel controlled, HR 78–82% max
  • 10K51:30–52:00 — settle into rhythm, take gel if practiced
  • 15K1:17:30–1:18:00 — hardest mental section; hold pace, do not surge
  • Finish1:44:30–1:45:00 — final 2K at whatever remains

Wrist pace smoothing lags on crowded starts — use km splits, not instantaneous pace. A chest strap helps in the first 3K when adrenaline spikes HR 10+ bpm above sustainable race effort.

When to Adjust

Miss a week due to illness or travel? Repeat the previous week rather than cramming missed miles. Persistent fatigue, elevated resting heart rate, or nagging pain means an extra recovery day — not another hard session.

FAQ

Can I run this plan on 4 days per week instead of 5?

Yes — drop one easy weekday run and keep the long run, tempo, and one other easy day. Expect slightly less volume tolerance; do not add missed miles to the long run (keep long run increases to 10 minutes per week max).

What if my tempo pace feels too hard by week 6?

Re-test goal pace with the Race Predictor using any recent race or time trial. Slow tempo by 5–8 sec/km and rebuild. Hitting tempo HR ceiling before 20 minutes means the target pace is above current threshold — not a willpower problem.

Should I cross-train during this plan?

Light cycling or swimming on rest days is fine if it replaces nothing. Hard CrossFit or heavy leg strength on days before tempo or long runs compromises key sessions. If you do both, treat running quality days as non-negotiable and schedule strength 24+ hours after the long run.

How do I know taper is working?

Resting HR should return to baseline or 1–2 bpm below. Legs feel springy on short strides. Easy runs at lower HR than build phase at the same pace. If you feel flat in race week, trust the taper — one short sharp session (week 12 Wednesday) is enough neuromuscular reminder.
