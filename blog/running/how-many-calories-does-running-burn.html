How Many Calories Does Running Burn?

Updated July 2026 · 11 min read

"How many calories did I burn?" is one of the first questions runners ask after a workout. It sounds simple, but the true answer depends on body weight, pace, terrain, efficiency, and even weather. Two runners can cover the same distance at the same speed and still get different numbers. That does not make calorie tracking useless; it just means estimates should be treated as ranges, not exact lab results.

This guide explains calorie burn by distance, introduces MET-based calculations, and shows what drives the biggest differences in real life. You will also learn how to use practical tools to estimate your own energy expenditure more accurately.

The Simple Rule of Thumb by Distance

For steady running on level ground, a practical estimate is that runners burn roughly 1 kcal per kilogram of body weight per kilometer. In imperial units, that is close to 0.75-1.0 kcal per pound per mile. This approximation is popular because it is easy and surprisingly useful over typical training distances.

Example: if you weigh 70 kg and run 10 km, a rough estimate is about 700 kcal. If you weigh 85 kg and run the same route, the estimate rises to about 850 kcal. This approach already captures one major driver: moving more body mass requires more energy.

However, this shortcut does not account for hills, hard surges, wind resistance, or major pace changes. For those factors, MET-based calculations offer more detail.

What MET Values Mean in Running

MET stands for Metabolic Equivalent of Task. One MET is your resting metabolic rate. Activities are assigned higher MET values based on intensity. The standard formula is:

Calories burned per minute = (MET x body weight in kg x 3.5) / 200

If you run for 45 minutes at a MET of 10 and weigh 70 kg, the estimate is about 551 kcal. Increase intensity to MET 12 and calorie burn rises accordingly.

Typical running MET ranges

  • Jogging (~8 km/h): around 8.3 MET
  • Easy running (~9.7 km/h): around 9.8 MET
  • Moderate running (~10.8 km/h): around 10.5 MET
  • Tempo running (~12 km/h): around 11.5-12.0 MET
  • Faster efforts (13+ km/h): 12.5 MET and above

These values are population averages. They are useful for planning, but no table can perfectly match your individual biomechanics and efficiency.

Calories Burned by Common Distances

Below are broad ranges using practical assumptions for steady outdoor running. Numbers vary by pace and conditions, but these benchmarks help with planning weekly training and nutrition.

5K running calorie estimate

Many runners burn about 250-450 kcal over a 5K. Lighter and highly efficient runners will be near the lower end; heavier runners and hillier routes push estimates upward.

10K running calorie estimate

A typical range is 500-900 kcal. For many recreational runners, 10K is long enough that hydration and fueling before the run can affect pace consistency, which in turn influences calorie output.

Half marathon calorie estimate

Most runners burn approximately 1100-2000 kcal in a half marathon depending on body mass, terrain, and finishing time. This is one reason fueling practice becomes important during longer training blocks.

Marathon calorie estimate

Marathon energy expenditure often lands around 2200-4000+ kcal. At this duration, glycogen depletion, heat stress, and pacing errors strongly affect performance, even when total calories burned looks similar between runners.

Why Two Devices Give Different Numbers

It is common to see a smartwatch, treadmill, and online calculator produce three different calorie estimates for the same run. The methods differ:

  • Some devices rely mostly on speed and body weight
  • Some incorporate heart-rate data and use proprietary models
  • Some include resting calories in the total while others report active calories only

The best approach is consistency. Use the same tracking method over time and watch trends rather than chasing perfect single-run precision.

Main Factors That Change Running Calorie Burn

Body weight and composition

Body mass has one of the largest effects on energy expenditure. More mass generally means more work per step. That said, body composition and movement efficiency influence how that energy is spent.

Pace and intensity distribution

Higher intensity increases calories burned per minute, but not always calories burned per kilometer in a perfectly linear way. Interval runs can increase post-exercise oxygen consumption, adding a small amount of extra energy use after the workout.

Terrain and elevation

Hills increase energy cost quickly. Trail running on uneven surfaces also raises muscular demand versus flat roads, especially when footing is technical.

Wind and temperature

Headwinds require more mechanical work. Heat and humidity can raise heart rate at a given pace, while very cold conditions increase thermoregulation demands.

Running economy

Better economy means using less oxygen at a given pace. As runners get fitter and technically smoother, they may run the same speed with slightly lower energy cost than before.

Calorie Burn and Fat Loss: What Runners Should Know

Running helps create an energy deficit, but fat loss is not a simple "exercise calories minus food calories" equation. Appetite changes, recovery needs, sleep, and stress all affect outcomes. Many runners overestimate workout calories and unintentionally eat back more than they expend.

If body-composition change is your goal, combine training with sustainable nutrition habits and realistic weekly expectations. Prioritize performance and recovery first. Aggressive calorie restriction often leads to low energy availability, poor workouts, and higher injury risk.

How to Estimate Your Own Calorie Burn Better

A practical method is to compare three sources and use a range:

  • Distance-based estimate (1 kcal/kg/km shortcut)
  • MET-based estimate from pace and duration
  • Device estimate from your watch/app

If all three are reasonably close, use the middle value. If they differ significantly, trust your long-term trend and adjust based on body weight, performance, and recovery over several weeks rather than single sessions.

Practical Takeaways

  • Distance gives a useful first estimate for running calories
  • MET formulas add detail when pace and duration are known
  • No calculator is perfect; treat numbers as informed ranges
  • Use one tracking system consistently to monitor trends
  • Fueling and recovery quality matter as much as calorie totals

Running calorie data is most helpful when it supports better decisions: pacing easy runs correctly, fueling longer sessions, and planning training load week to week. Use the numbers as guidance, then let consistent performance and recovery tell you whether your plan is working.

Worked Example: Planning a Week of Running Calories

Profile: 72 kg runner, steady outdoor pace 5:45/km (~10.4 km/h), training four days per week.

Tuesday — 45 min easy: Distance-based estimate: 72 kcal/km × 7.8 km ≈ 562 kcal. MET estimate at 9.8 MET: (9.8 × 72 × 3.5 / 200) × 45 ≈ 556 kcal. Reasonable planning range: 540–580 kcal.

Thursday — 35 min with hills: Same pace on paper, but elevation adds cost. Distance estimate still ~6.0 km → ~432 kcal. MET at 10.5 with hill adjustment → ~480 kcal. Use the higher end for nutrition planning on hilly days.

Saturday — 75 min long run: ~13 km at easy pace → ~936 kcal by distance rule. MET at 9.8 for 75 min → ~926 kcal. Weekly total from running alone: roughly 1,900–2,100 kcal before counting daily steps and strength work.

Decision rule: If your Garmin reports 2,400 kcal for the same week, check whether it includes resting calories in the activity total. For deficit planning, use active calories only and compare against food intake trends over 14 days — not a single Saturday long run.

Expert Note

The 1 kcal/kg/km shortcut assumes level ground and steady pace. It systematically undercounts hilly trail runs and overcounts treadmill sessions where the belt assists slightly. For weight-loss planning, bias toward the distance-based estimate on flat routes and add 10–15% on courses with more than 150 m/km of climbing. Pair with the Running Calories Calculator rather than treating any one device reading as exact.

Garmin and COROS Calorie Estimate Limitations

Both Garmin (Firstbeat) and COROS use proprietary models that blend speed, heart rate, user profile data, and sometimes power or running dynamics. They often produce different totals for the same run — differences of 15–25% are common.

  • Active vs total calories: Garmin Connect may show "total calories" that include basal metabolic rate during the activity window. COROS typically separates active burn more clearly. Always verify which field you are reading before logging food.
  • Heart rate dependency: Wrist optical HR lags during intervals and spikes in heat. When HR is wrong, calorie models that weight HR heavily will also be wrong — often overstating easy runs and understating true hard efforts.
  • Running economy blind spot: Neither platform knows your biomechanical efficiency. Two runners at identical pace and weight can differ by 5–10% in actual oxygen cost. Watch estimates assume population averages.
  • Post-run EPOC: Device "calories" usually end when you press stop. The small afterburn from hard sessions is not fully captured — but it is also smaller than most runners assume (often 50–80 kcal, not 300).

Best practice: pick one platform, track weekly running calorie trends, and validate against scale weight and performance every two to three weeks. If the watch and MET estimate diverge by more than 20%, trust the range midpoint for planning — not the higher number that makes refueling feel justified.

FAQ

Does running faster burn more calories per mile?

Yes, but not proportionally. A 10% pace increase might raise per-minute burn by 10–15% while shortening duration. Over a fixed distance, faster running burns slightly more total calories — but the difference is smaller than most people expect. Volume and consistency matter more than turning every run into a sprint.

Should I eat back all the calories my watch reports?

No. Wearables routinely overestimate burn, and appetite often rises after hard sessions anyway. Use watch calories as a rough weekly expenditure input, not a license to match intake meal for meal. Track 7-day weight trends instead.

Why does my treadmill show fewer calories than my Garmin for the same pace?

Treadmill algorithms often use simpler speed-and-weight formulas without HR. Outdoor GPS watches add elevation, temperature, and HR adjustments. Neither is ground truth — compare both to a MET estimate and use the middle value.

Is walking the same distance equal to running for calorie burn?

No. Running has a higher MET value per minute because both feet leave the ground, increasing mechanical work. Walking 5 km burns roughly 60–70% of what running 5 km burns for the same person. Running remains more time-efficient for energy expenditure.
