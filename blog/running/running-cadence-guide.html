Running Cadence: Optimal Steps and Drills

Updated July 2026 · 10 min read

Cadence is how many steps you take per minute (spm). It's one of the simplest levers for improving running economy and reducing injury risk. Most recreational runners overstride at low cadence; increasing step rate toward 170–180 spm often produces a lighter, more efficient stride without forcing speed.

Why Cadence Matters

Low cadence usually means longer strides and heavier heel striking — more braking force with each footfall. Higher cadence shortens stride length, lands feet closer under your center of mass, and reduces impact stress on knees and hips.

  • Lower impact forces — Shorter, quicker steps absorb less shock per stride
  • Better running economy — Less vertical bounce, more forward momentum
  • Reduced overstriding — Feet land under hips instead of ahead of the body

The 170–180 Target

Research and coaching consensus point to 170–180 spm as an efficient range for most distance runners. This isn't a magic number — elite runners often exceed 180 at race pace — but it's a practical target for recreational athletes currently below 165.

Increase cadence by 5–10% gradually. Jumping from 155 to 180 overnight creates new stress on calves and Achilles tendons. Give your body 4–6 weeks to adapt.

How to Measure Cadence

  1. Wrist or foot pod: Most GPS watches report cadence automatically during runs
  2. Manual count: Count right-foot strikes for 30 seconds, multiply by 2 (or count both feet and use as-is for 30 sec × 2)
  3. Metronome app: Set to 170–175 bpm and match footfalls to the beat during easy runs

Drills to Improve Cadence

Add these after an easy warm-up, 2–3 times per week. Each drill: 2–3 × 20–30 meters with walk-back recovery.

  • A-skipsDrive knee up, paw the ground under your hips
  • B-skipsExtend knee forward, then pull through (hamstring activation)
  • High kneesQuick turnover, not slow exaggerated lifts
  • Butt kicksHeels to glutes, fast feet
  • Strides4–6 × 80–100m at 5K effort, focus on quick light steps

Cadence During Easy Runs

Practice higher cadence on easy days first — not during tempo or long runs until it feels natural. Use a metronome at 172 bpm for 5-minute segments within a 30-minute easy run. Check your pace with the Pace Calculator to confirm you're not speeding up; cadence and pace are independent.

Cadence vs. Speed

As you run faster, cadence naturally increases. Easy runs might sit at 170 spm while 5K pace pushes 180+. Don't chase a fixed number at every intensity — focus on avoiding plodding below 165 at easy pace.

When Not to Obsess Over Cadence

Cadence is a symptom of good form, not the sole cause. If you're injury-free and running well, a cadence of 168 isn't a problem. Prioritize easy-day discipline and strength work alongside cadence drills. Pair with our easy run pace guide for a complete form-and-effort approach.

Worked Example: Raising Cadence Without Speeding Up

Profile: Female, age 34, easy pace 6:10/km, 5K PR 24:45. Initial cadence on flat treadmill at easy effort: 158 spm (Garmin Forerunner 255, wrist optical). Goal: reach 170 spm at the same pace and heart rate (zone 2, 138–148 bpm per Zone 2 Calculator).

Week 1–2: Metronome set to 165 bpm during minutes 10–25 of a 40-minute easy run. Average pace slips from 6:10 to 6:02/km — classic cadence trap. Correction: hold treadmill speed fixed at 9.7 km/h, accept awkward feel, HR stays 142 bpm. Cadence averages 164 spm.

Week 4: Outdoor run, same 6:10/km pace verified with Pace Calculator lap splits. Cadence 169 spm, stride length down ~8% (from ~1.05 m to ~0.97 m). Vertical oscillation on Garmin drops from 9.2 cm to 7.8 cm — indirect sign of reduced braking.

Week 6: Cadence holds 171 spm at 6:08/km easy pace without metronome. Calf soreness from week 2 resolved after reducing drill volume to 2× per week. 5K time trial unchanged at 24:50 — expected, since cadence work targets economy and injury risk, not VO2 max. Garmin, COROS, and Cadence Data: Trust but Verify

Both Garmin and COROS derive cadence from accelerometer peaks at foot strike. Treadmill runs often read 2–4 spm higher than overground at the same effort because belt assist changes impact timing. Trail runs with hiking sections can report spm below 140 when you're walking — polluting run averages if you don't lap-segment.

Wrist vs foot pod: Wrist-based cadence on Garmin is generally reliable at steady paces above 8:00/mile (5:00/km). During intervals, wrist cadence can double-count arm swing on faster reps, inflating spm by 5–8. COROS foot pod (POD 2) or Garmin Running Dynamics Pod gives ground-contact-time symmetry and vertical ratio — more useful for form feedback than spm alone.

Live vs average: Post-run average cadence hides surges. A runner averaging 172 spm might spend minutes at 160 on uphills and 182 on flats. Check lap cadence or enable a live cadence data field; target consistency within ±5 spm on uniform terrain.

Running power (COROS/Garmin): Power rises when you increase cadence at fixed pace on flats — shorter steps often mean more steps per minute at similar metabolic cost, but power also captures wind and grade. Don't use power to "prove" cadence gains; use pace-at-same-HR from the HR Zones Calculator as the economy check over 4–6 weeks.

Expert Note

Forced cadence increases without pace control are the fastest way to turn easy runs into moderate runs. If HR climbs 8+ bpm at the same pace after a cadence block, you shortened stride too aggressively. Back off 3–5 spm until HR normalizes — economy gains require weeks, not one metronome session.

FAQ

Does cadence matter on hills?

Yes, but targets shift. Uphill cadence often rises naturally (175–185 spm) with shorter steps; downhill tempts overstriding and cadence drops. Focus on quick light steps downhill rather than hitting 170 on a 6% grade. Compare cadence on similar segments week to week, not flat vs hill.

Can cadence fixes replace strength training?

No. Cadence drills improve neuromuscular timing; they don't build the hip and calf capacity to sustain higher turnover. Single-leg calf raises, hip abduction, and glute bridges support cadence changes. Runners who drill A-skips daily but skip strength work often get Achilles flare-ups at 170+ spm.

My watch shows cadence in steps per minute per foot — which number do I use?

Running convention is total steps (both feet) per minute — what Garmin and COROS display by default. Some apps report single-leg cadence; double it for comparison to the 170–180 range. When in doubt, count both feet for 30 seconds and multiply by 2.

Cadence vs Pace Trade-off

Increasing cadence without controlling pace is the most common form mistake in recreational running. Higher step rate often shortens stride — which can reduce braking forces — but if pace creeps faster, heart rate climbs and the session stops being an easy aerobic run. Cadence work only counts when pace and effort stay fixed while spm rises.

Data scenario — same runner, same 45-minute easy run, flat loop, 16°C, chest strap HR:

  • Session A (180 spm shuffle): Forced 180 spm via metronome. Pace accelerates from 6:00/km to 5:42/km by minute 20. Average cadence 179 spm. Average HR 152 bpm — zone 3, not zone 2. Vertical oscillation drops (good), but metabolic cost rises (bad for an easy day). Garmin reports "improved form"; the aerobic purpose of the run failed.
  • Session B (168 spm efficient): Natural cadence after 6 weeks of gradual work. Pace held at 6:02/km throughout. Average cadence 168 spm. Average HR 142 bpm — true zone 2 per the Zone 2 Calculator. Stride length 0.98 m vs 0.89 m in Session A. Slightly lower spm, but pace-at-same-HR is the economy win.

How to read the trade-off: Session A looks better on a cadence dashboard. Session B builds aerobic base and recovery capacity. At 168 spm with stable HR, this runner is efficient enough — chasing 180 spm on every easy run would add calf load without proportional benefit. Compare pace-at-140-bpm monthly, not spm alone.

Controlled experiment protocol: Pick a flat segment. Lock target pace with lap alerts or treadmill speed. Record cadence, HR, and RPE at minutes 10, 25, and 40. Change only cadence variable (metronome ±5 spm) between sessions. Success = same pace ±3 sec/km, same HR ±3 bpm, cadence +5 spm vs baseline after 4 weeks.

At race pace, cadence naturally rises — a 5K at 178 spm does not mean easy runs must also hit 178. Use the Pace Calculator to set pace anchors before any cadence block so you are measuring stride mechanics, not disguised speed work.

Expert Note

COROS and Garmin both report cadence and pace on the same screen — the trap is optimizing the number that updates fastest. spm responds within seconds; aerobic drift takes 30 minutes to reveal. Hold pace fixed for the first month of cadence work even if 172 spm feels "too low" compared to social media form advice.
